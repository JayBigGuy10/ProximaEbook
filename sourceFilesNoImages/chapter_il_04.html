Interlude IV: A Night on Earth

 

Johnson Space Center
Houston, Texas, USA
4:30 PM Central Time

Anna sat quietly in the front row of the auditorium in Building 2, a place she had come to know all too well. The Houston weather was dreary this afternoon, it had swung from oppressively hot to a torrential downpour in a matter of hours, typical. She reveled in the fact that she did not need to be outside, and that her car was close by. The time of the conference starting had come and gone, she checked her watch, eager to hear what the presenters had to say. This press conference had been nearly 20 years in the making, finally paying off with one grand celebration. Jonathan “Jack” Kissner, the new head of Astrophysics, stepped on stage. This was nerve racking for him, clearly, and Anna watched as the beads of sweat began to pool on his forehead. First rodeo, Anna thought, shifting in her seat, offering a kind smile to Kissner. Rather nervously, he stepped up to the microphone, tapping it gently, and cleared his throat before proceeding.

“Ladies and Gentlemen, good morning. As you all know, for many years, we have been pondering the name of what our next giant leap in the field of astronomy is going to be… and I have the absolute honor of being able to tell you. I stand here today to introduce to you the next revolutionary advancement in international astronomy, one that will enable us to ask new questions about our dynamic universe. But first, I want to discuss some history. Over 160 years ago, a pioneering American woman made the perilous journey to freedom from slavery. Harriet Tubman, one of the bravest women in history, did something extraordinary. Under the cover of night, and using the stars as her guide, she went back. She made the journey on the Underground Railroad over 13 times to rescue those in need and guide them to freedom, and never lost a passenger. Today, we look to the future with the stars as our guide once again, and hope to reflect on our past. With the Harriet Tubman Space Telescope, we will look to the past to inform our questions about our future, and honor the legacy of all of those who had the courage to look to the stars. With this new flagship of astronomy, we will do more than look to the stars, we will ponder the very nature our universe, and blaze a trail forwards towards a greater understanding. As of now, we are tracking a launch for the telescope no later than December, 2017... I'd like to thank you all for your time.”

The room erupted, and Anna felt herself sink into her seat, admiring the work of this bold young leader. For many years, there had been much internal debate as to what to call this telescope, a powerful symbol of the advancements that international spaceflight was taking. When Anna had joined the corps, it had seemed nearly impossible that a black woman would be commanding the first mission to Mars, in the culture of ex-Air Force hot shots that dominated the field. But now, it would seem, space exploration had made considerable strides in including all - a world that was moving perhaps beyond the prejudices of her youth, towards a greater cosmic understanding that perhaps meant that a united future was possible. It, for a moment, reminded her of her own age, her own mortality. She had seen such massive change in such a short period of time. She was getting up there in years for the Astronaut Corps, and had wondered quietly if her flight to Horizon would be her last great hurrah… But that didn’t matter now, she had much work to do, stepping up on stage to shake Kissner’s hand and congratulate him on this momentous achievement. He seemed to be more flustered than when he began his speech, not something your every day director could handle. He nervously made small talk, trying his best not to be starstruck in the presence of the Commander. She smiled, and congratulated him on this most historic day. She lingered around the room for some time, admiring the handshakes and embraces, a reminder of the human element that made up this great agency she had called home for so many years. As the evening drew to a close, she found herself walking through the parking lot, still slick with rain - but she didn’t mind - for the sun was shining in Houston once again.

“It is not so much the rule itself, but the fact that they are even asking. This is a rare moment in time.”

“Anna.”

“I don’t know, Sergei… I don’t know what to do… I cannot move mountains, and I don’t know if I can handle another rescue operation.”

“Anna!”

“Do the Russians know more? I need more time, more information…”

“Anna, listen to me. I am asking you to trust yourself, trust me, and extend a hand to trust them. This is a time critical situation, and I know that you are more than capable of handling this. You saved my life, and I wouldn’t trust anyone else to do the same.”

“Sergei…”

“They need you, they need all of us. I have Doctor Liao on the phone, she’s my point of contact. She wants to speak to you directly.”

“Okay. Thank you Sergei.”

“Thank you Anna. I’ll see you in a few hours.”
