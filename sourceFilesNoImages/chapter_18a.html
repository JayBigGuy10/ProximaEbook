Chapter 18: Annex

 

Hi all!, Time for some amazing images provided by our amazing artists, who I'm so grateful to feature in this week's chapter! Once again I'd like to thank Jay, Zarbon and Ben for doing these fantastic pieces of work, and helping to illustrate the universe of Proxima as a whole. Today, we'll be looking at images of the first arrival operations at Mars, with the uncrewed base station doing its capture burn as we prepare for the eventual crew arrival (as well as some bonus goodies that I've thrown in here). As I've thought about this architecture more and more, the more that something like this (with a good amount of pre-staging) makes sense. It gives me hope that the lessons learned during Lunar operations with Artemis will give us a good shot at getting to Mars successfully and sustainably.


The Mars Base Station begins the long fall towards the Red Planet, ready to ignite her Valkyrie nuclear engines and make orbit.


With the station entering the correct attitude, the nuclear engines are given the command by the flight computer to fire, starting the process of slowing down from interplanetary velocity.


Planetshine from Mars illuminates the vehicle for the first time as it makes orbit, the Valkyries working hard to slow the vehicle into a stable orbit.


Drop tank separation, as their added mass is no longer needed. These tanks remain on an interplanetary trajectory, to be thrown by Mars' gravity into a heliocentric orbit.​


The first healthy signals from Mars are received, and the crew of Olympus 3 can relax knowing that their destination station is right where it is supposed to be. ​


The base station takes in some sweeping vistas of Olympus Mons.


Logistics Lander 1 "Marie Curie" prepares to make her descent to the planet's surface after casting off her cruise stage.


The roughest part of EDL over, she can now get ready to fire her engines to bring her to a gentle stop.


NASA promotional image highlighting the upcoming Olympus 3 mission, their Spot the Fleet campaign, and the collaborations with news agencies to broadcast information about the flight to the public.​
